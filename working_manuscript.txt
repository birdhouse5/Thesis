Dataset Description: This study employs daily price and volume data for a diversified portfolio of 30 S&P 500 companies spanning the period from 1990 to 2025, providing 35 years of continuous market data without survivorship bias interruptions. The selected companies represent a cross-sectional sample of the U.S. equity market, with representation across nine major sectors: Technology (6 companies: IBM, MSFT, ORCL, INTC, HPQ, CSCO), Financial Services (5 companies: JPM, BAC, WFC, C, AXP), Healthcare (4 companies: JNJ, PFE, MRK, ABT), Consumer Staples (4 companies: KO, PG, WMT, PEP), Energy (3 companies: XOM, CVX, COP), Industrials (3 companies: GE, CAT, BA), Consumer Discretionary (2 companies: HD, MCD), Utilities (2 companies: SO, D), and Materials (1 company: DD). These companies were specifically chosen for their continuous listing on the S&P 500 since 1990, ensuring data integrity and avoiding gaps from delistings, mergers, or major corporate restructuring events. The selection criteria prioritize long-term survivors that have demonstrated exceptional resilience through multiple economic cycles, with research indicating that such survivor stocks tend to outperform broader market indices while exhibiting lower exposure to tail risks (https://www.mdpi.com/1911-8074/15/2/95, https://www.qad.com/blog/2019/10/sp-500-companies-over-time). This dataset provides sufficient diversity in correlation structures, volatility patterns, and factor exposures necessary for robust reinforcement learning model training in portfolio optimization applications.

Data Preparation and Feature Engineering
The dataset underwent comprehensive preprocessing to ensure optimal performance for reinforcement learning-based portfolio optimization. We computed 22 technical indicators including trend-following indicators (MACD, moving averages), momentum oscillators (RSI, Stochastic, Williams %R), volatility measures (ATR, NATR, Bollinger Bands), and volume-based indicators (MFI, VWAP) using robust implementations that handle edge cases such as division-by-zero and extreme values through clipping and fallback mechanisms. Given the heterogeneous scales of financial indicators (e.g., RSI bounded [0,100], MACD unbounded, ATR scale-dependent), we applied differentiated normalization strategies: MinMax scaling for price-based features to preserve relative movements within each asset, simple ratio scaling for naturally bounded oscillators, robust standardization using median and interquartile range for unbounded oscillators to mitigate outlier effects, and log-transformation followed by standardization for positively skewed volatility measures. Additionally, we engineered RL-specific features including daily returns, log-returns, excess returns relative to an equal-weighted market benchmark, and rolling volatility estimates (5-day and 20-day windows) to provide the agent with explicit risk and performance metrics. All normalization was performed per ticker to preserve cross-sectional heterogeneity while ensuring temporal consistency, resulting in a standardized feature space suitable for neural network-based RL algorithms.

Given the non-stationary nature of financial time series with changing inter-asset correlations and non-constant variances, these features provide essential regime information for the variBAD framework. Specifically, correlation-based features capture the phenomenon observed during financial crises, where increased inter-asset correlation signals market stress and requires strategic portfolio hedging. Volatility-based features complement this by detecting periods of heightened market uncertainty. This design leverages Markowitz's insight regarding the importance of asset correlations in risk-return optimization while providing the temporal regime information necessary for variBAD's posterior belief updating mechanism.

Missing Value Treatment: The dataset contained 744 missing values (0.01% of total data) concentrated in the initial trading periods, consistent with the warmup requirements of technical indicators and rolling window calculations. Missing values occurred systematically across all securities for features requiring historical lookback periods: Rate of Change indicators (300 values, requiring 10-day history), RSI calculations (84 values, requiring 14-day periods), and rolling volatility measures (60 values each for 5-day and 20-day windows). We applied feature-appropriate imputation strategies: zero-filling for return calculations where no prior comparison exists, neutral values for bounded oscillators (RSI normalized to 0.5, representing the midpoint of the 0-100 scale), and backward propagation for volatility measures to utilize the first available statistical estimate. This conservative approach preserves the mathematical integrity of technical indicators while avoiding data invention, ensuring that the initial model training periods reflect realistic market entry conditions rather than artificially constructed values.
